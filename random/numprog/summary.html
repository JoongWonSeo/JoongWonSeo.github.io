1. Floating Point Numbers

Discretization of R\RR, without the disadvantages of fixed point (fixed range, overflow, too little precision at small numbers and wasted precision at big numbers, ...)

Normalized ttt-digit floating point to basis BBB (machine number):

FB,t,α,β={M⋅BE∣M=0∨Bt−1≤∣M∣<Bt⏟⇒ t digits, no leading 0, α≤E≤β⏟range for E, with M,E∈Z}\mathbb{F}_{B,t,\alpha,\beta} = \{ M \cdot B^E \mid M=0 \lor \underbrace{B^{t-1} \leq |M| < B^t}_{\Rightarrow\ t \text{ digits, no leading 0}},\ \underbrace{\alpha \leq E \leq \beta}_{\text{range for E}}, \text{ with } M, E \in \Z \}FB,t,α,β​={M⋅BE∣M=0∨⇒ t digits, no leading 0Bt−1≤∣M∣<Bt​​, range for Eα≤E≤β​​, with M,E∈Z}

The mantissa MMM and exponent EEE are saved as integers, the basis BBB and digits ttt are ususally implicit.

The absolute distance between two neighboring floats is not constant:
∣98⋅100−99⋅100∣=100\qquad |98 \cdot 10^0 - 99 \cdot 10^0| = 10^0∣98⋅100−99⋅100∣=100
∣98⋅101−99⋅101∣=101\qquad |98 \cdot 10^1 - 99 \cdot 10^1| = 10^1∣98⋅101−99⋅101∣=101
The bigger the absolute value gets, the less precision it has (logarithmic scale!)
